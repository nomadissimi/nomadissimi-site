# **Chapter 2 What Is a Codice Fiscale?**

<GuideSoftTeal>

The _Codice Fiscale_ literally translates to “Fiscal Code,” but it’s much more useful to think of it as your ultimate Italian access pass.
</GuideSoftTeal>

<GuideSpacer size="sm" />

## **How Your Codice Fiscale is Built**

You might also hear people call it a “Tax Code,” but honestly, that makes it sound way more intimidating than it actually is.

Instead of thinking about taxes, think of the Codice Fiscale as your ultimate Italian access pass. If you are coming from the US, it is _similar_ to your Social Security Number (SSN).  
**It is a 16-character alphanumeric code that uniquely identifies you as an individual in Italy**. Everyone breathing and living legally in Italy gets one.

<GuideSoftTeal title="Not a coincidence">

_Here’s the cool part:_ it is not just a randomly assigned string of letters and numbers that you’ll never remember. The Codice Fiscale is actually generated directly from your personal information using a very specific formula.

</GuideSoftTeal>

The 16 characters are built like this:

<GuideChecklist title="How the 16 characters are built">

- **Your surname** — usually the first 3 consonants
- **Your first name** — usually the first 3 consonants
- **Your birth year** — last 2 digits
- **Your birth month** — 1 letter (each month has a specific letter assigned)
- **Your birthday and gender** — 2 digits (women get 40 added to their birthday number)
- **Your place of birth** — 4 alphanumeric characters from a special registry code; foreign countries have their own unique codes starting with `"Z"`
- **A control character** — 1 final letter calculated from an algorithm based on all the previous characters

</GuideChecklist>

<GuideTaxSnapshot
title="Example"
subtitle="It looks complicated at first, but once you see the pattern, it starts to make sense."

>

_Let’s look at an example._ If your name is Charlotte Smith, born in New York on January 1st, 1990, your Codice Fiscale might look something like this:

**SMTCRL90A41Z404W**

- `SMT` for Smith
- `CRL` for Charlotte
- `90` for the year
- `A` for January
- `41` because `01 + 40` for a female
- `Z404` for the USA
- and a final control letter

</GuideTaxSnapshot>

Once you understand the formula, you will probably be able to recite your code by heart\!

### **What Makes It Different from Other ID Numbers?**

If you're American, you might be thinking, "So it's just like my SSN?" Not really. While both serve as unique identifiers, the _codice fiscale_ is used far more broadly in daily Italian life. In the US, you rarely need to show your SSN outside of employment, taxes, or banking. In Italy, you'll need your codice fiscale for almost everything: from signing a lease to buying a SIM card.

<GuideBlueNote title="Very important distinction">

Also, **having a codice fiscale does NOT automatically create any tax obligations**. You're only subject to Italian taxes if you meet specific criteria: owning certain assets in Italy, being an Italian tax resident, or earning Italian taxable income. Simply having the code doesn't mean Italy expects you to start paying taxes automatically.

</GuideBlueNote>

# **Chapter 3 What Can You Use It For?**

If you are wondering why we are making such a big deal out of a 16-character code, it is because your Codice Fiscale is the master key to your new life in Italy. The Italian system is incredibly interconnected, and this code is the thread that ties all your legal, financial, and daily activities together.

Here is what it unlocks for you on a practical level.

## **1. Setting Up Your Life (Housing, Banking, and Bills)**

<GuideOfficeCard>

**The Codice Fiscale is the very first thing you will need to establish yourself as a resident.**  
If you want to open an Italian bank account so you can pay your rent without insane international transfer fees, the bank will ask for your CF.

If you want to sign a rental lease, landlords are legally required to register residential contracts with the _Agenzia delle Entrate_ (revenue agency). They usually do it with the codice fiscale.

You will need the codice fiscale to activate the electricity, set up the gas, and get high-speed internet installed. Even something as simple as walking into a WindTre or TIM store usually requires you to hand over your Codice Fiscale.

</GuideOfficeCard>

## **2. All Things Healthcare (Public, Private, and Pharmacy)**

<GuideSoftTeal title="Yes, healthcare too">

Here is something that surprises a lot of immigrants in Italy: **the Codice Fiscale is deeply tied to the Italian healthcare system**, regardless of whether you are using public or private services.

Whenever you go to a private doctor, book a specialist visit, see a psychologist, or even buy a box of painkillers or a specific cream at the local pharmacy (_farmacia_), they will ask for your Codice Fiscale so they can issue a proper receipt.

</GuideSoftTeal>

<GuideBigSis title="A quick side note on Italian healthcare">

When we talk about paying for “medical expenses” in Italy, we need to clarify what that means, because it is probably completely different from what you might be used to. If you are coming from the US and the idea of calling an ambulance gives you financial anxiety, you can officially let that go.

If you are in a genuine emergency and an ambulance takes you to the public hospital ER (_Pronto Soccorso_), that life-saving care (the ride, the IV, the doctors, the days in the hospital) is generally free of charge, even for foreigners. **Zero outrageous bills in the mail.**

When we talk about “paying out of pocket” in Italy, we are referring to standard checkups, private specialists, routine blood tests, and pharmacy medications. And even then, you will quickly discover that medical treatments and prescriptions in Italy have more reasonable prices. Your Codice Fiscale is simply what tracks those payments so you can get tax deductions on them later.

</GuideBigSis>

## **3. Unlocking Further Tax Benefits**

Here is where having a Codice Fiscale actually works in your favor. **Even though simply having the code doesn't automatically mean you owe taxes, it _does_ allow you to claim tax deductions later on if you become an Italian tax resident.**

<GuideQuickFacts title="Why receipts matter">

_Remember those receipts from the pharmacy, the dentist, or the private medical exams?_ If you give the provider your codice fiscale, the purchase is officially recorded.

Keep those receipts. Perhaps get a folder dedicated to your medical receipts (even those receipts for your migraine medicines). They all add up.

When tax season rolls around, you can use those accumulated medical expenses to lower your taxable income\!  
The same goes for things like charitable donations or certain insurance premiums.

</GuideQuickFacts>

<GuideMistakeBox title="Big-sis tip">

To claim these tax deductions, the Italian government requires a certain “digital paper trail.” That means you must pay for your medical expenses or pharmacy runs with a credit or debit card (no cash), provide your Codice Fiscale, and save the receipt.

We discuss in more details exactly how these deductions work in our _Tax & Partita IVA Consultation_, but for now, just make it a habit: _always pay by card, always give your Codice Fiscale, and always keep the receipt\!_

</GuideMistakeBox>

## **4. Life’s Big Bureaucratic Milestones**

<GuideDecisionBox>

Beyond your daily errands, the codice fiscale is the anchor for all your official paperwork.

If you eventually go to the town hall _(Comune)_ to register your residency or apply for your Italian ID card (CIE: _Carta di Identità Elettronica_), your codice fiscale is required.

If you decide to buy a Vespa to zip around town, or a countryside villa, your codice fiscale goes on the deed.

Regardless of whether you are signing an employment contract, a freelance agreement, or just a long-term gym membership, your codice fiscale is the signature that makes it legally visible inside Italy’s system.

</GuideDecisionBox>

# **Chapter 4 Codice Fiscale vs. Partita IVA**

This is the exact spot where most newcomers to Italy get completely tangled up in translation, so let’s clear it up right now.

<TealBadge>Important distinction</TealBadge>

<GuideSpacer size="sm" />

Because “_Codice Fiscale_” literally translates to “Fiscal Code,” a lot of people mistakenly believe it is a business tax number. It is not.

**The Codice Fiscale is completely different from the _Partita IVA_** (your Italian VAT Number).  
They serve two entirely different purposes in the eyes of the Italian government, and getting them confused can lead to serious headaches _(at least try to save the receipt for your headache medicine haha)._

Here is the easiest way to remember the difference:

## **Wait, so do I need both?**

That depends entirely on which visa you are holding.

<GuideChecklist title="Which one applies to you?">

- **If you are a Remote Worker (Employee):**  
  **You only need the Codice Fiscale.** Because you are working remotely for a foreign employer on a payroll, you are not running your own business in Italy. You most likely will never need to open a Partita IVA.

- **If you are a Digital Nomad (Freelancer):**  
  **You need both.** Because you are self-employed and handle your own taxes, the Italian government requires you to open a Partita IVA to legally work and invoice your clients.

</GuideChecklist>

## **The Catch: The CF Comes First**

If you are a freelancer, you cannot just skip straight to opening your Partita IVA. The Italian system literally will not let you. **The Codice Fiscale is an absolute, non-negotiable prerequisite to opening a business tax number.**

<GuideDecisionBox title="The order matters">

First, you register your existence as a person (_codice fiscale_), and only then can you register your existence as a business (_Partita IVA_).

</GuideDecisionBox>

# **Chapter 5 How and When to Get Your Codice Fiscale: the 3 Paths**

<TealBadge>Timing matters</TealBadge>

<GuideSpacer size="sm" />

Now that you know exactly how vital this 16-character code is for your daily life and transactions in Italy, the next logical question is: _how quickly can I get mine?_

The honest answer is that **it's better to have it as soon as possible**. But depending on where you are in your relocation timeline, there are three different ways this can play out.

Let's walk through them, starting with our absolute favorite strategy, down to the slowest option.

## **Path 1: Before You Move to Italy**

<GuideDecisionBox title="Our favorite route">

The earliest time to secure your _Codice Fiscale_ is while you are still sitting comfortably in your home country.  
**You can request it directly from the Italian Consulate or Embassy that handles your jurisdiction, ideally even before your visa application appointment.**  
_This is the route we recommend in most cases._

</GuideDecisionBox>

Imagine this: you land in Italy, exhausted but excited, and your Codice Fiscale is already tucked safely in your bag. This saves you a step.  
It means you can walk straight out of the airport to get a good Italian phone plan, or open a local bank account during your very first week, without any awkward pauses.

The reality is that no one is going to magically email you a Codice Fiscale just because you decided to move to Italy. You have to be the one to ask for it\!  
Because having this code makes securing your Italian housing so much easier (which is a step you need to do _before_ your visa appointment), getting your _codice fiscale_ should be one of the very first things you do.

### **The Action Plan**

Because this request must be made at the specific consulate in your jurisdiction (the exact same office that will eventually handle your visa), your goal is to figure out their specific, local procedure for issuing the codice fiscale to foreigners.

<GuideSteps title="Path 1 action plan">

**1. Discover your local consulate's rules**  
Every Italian office loves to operate slightly differently. Your first step is to go to your specific consulate’s website and search for their “Codice Fiscale” page if they have one, or reach out directly.

Look for how they accept requests from non-Italian citizens. Some consulates allow you to request it fully remotely via a simple email, others require you to mail physical documents, and a few might ask you to drop by during walk-in hours.

_For instance_, to give you an idea of what a clear procedure looks like, these are the Washington D.C. Embassy instructions:  
[https://ambwashingtondc.esteri.it/en/servizi-consolari-e-visti/servizi-per-il-cittadino-italiano/codice-fiscale/](https://ambwashingtondc.esteri.it/en/servizi-consolari-e-visti/servizi-per-il-cittadino-italiano/codice-fiscale/)

**2. Download the AA4/8 form**  
No matter which consulate you belong to, the core of the application is the exact same piece of paper: the AA4/8 form. You will probably find this document buried somewhere on your consulate's website, but to save you the bureaucratic treasure hunt, you can download the English-friendly, editable version directly from the Italian Revenue Agency [right here](https://www.agenziaentrate.gov.it/portale/documents/20143/278995/Form+AA48+Editable_AA4_8_Modello_ING.pdf/e32d806e-92e3-05a5-37ac-ab270ff86a74).

**3. Submit your request**  
Follow your consulate's specific instructions to the letter. Usually, this means digitally filling out the AA4/8 form, printing it, physically signing it, scanning it back in, and sending it off along with a scan of your passport and a utility bill to prove you live in their jurisdiction.

</GuideSteps>

<GuideBigSis title="Big-sis tip">

When the form asks for your reason for requesting a Codice Fiscale, keep it simple and honest. You can state that you need it to _"stipulate a residential lease agreement in Italy in preparation for your Digital Nomad Visa application."_  
The consulate knows this is how the system works\!

</GuideBigSis>

## **Path 2: Getting it Done on Italian Soil**

Maybe you did not know you could request the Codice Fiscale before you left, or maybe your local consulate was simply unresponsive. Or perhaps you just did not need it urgently while you were still in your home country. Whatever the reason, if you arrive in Italy without a Codice Fiscale, it is not the end of the world.

If you find yourself on Italian soil needing your code to open a standard bank account, buy a car, or set up utilities in your new home, but you cannot afford to wait months for the immigration office to issue it _(which we will cover in Path 3)_, you can take matters into your own hands.

<GuideSoftTeal>

As soon as you arrive, **you can head directly to any local branch of the _Agenzia delle Entrate_ (the Italian Revenue Agency) to request your code in person.**

</GuideSoftTeal>

### **The Action Plan**

Your goal here is to apply for your code and get out without letting the legendary Italian bureaucracy eat up your entire afternoon.

For official reference, you can read the Agenzia delle Entrate's exact requirements for foreign citizens on their [dedicated English page here](https://www.agenziaentrate.gov.it/portale/web/english/nse/individuals/tax-identification-number-for-foreign-citizens).

<GuideBigSis title="One translation note">

When you’re searching for official information on the Agenzia delle Entrate website, you will notice they translate Codice Fiscale into English as “Tax Identification Number.” As we clarified earlier, please do not let this scare you\! The name makes it sound like you are signing up to pay heavy taxes, but it truly is just your standard identification code for daily life in Italy.

</GuideBigSis>

<GuideSteps title="Path 2 action plan">

**1. Find Your Office and Book an Appointment**  
Do not just walk into a random Italian tax office unannounced. You will want to book an official appointment (_prenotazione_) to save yourself from standing in a chaotic queue or being sent home.

_Go to the official office-finder portal:_ [https://www.agenziaentrate.gov.it/portale/trova-ufficio](https://www.agenziaentrate.gov.it/portale/trova-ufficio)

From there, select your city, navigate to “_Servizi Fiscali_” (Tax Services), and look for the option to book an appointment for the first request of a Tax Identification Code.

**2. Assemble Your Mini Kit**  
When you get to the counter, the clerk is going to expect you to be prepared. You will need to bring:

- Your valid passport
- Your Italian Visa, which is physically stamped inside one of your passport pages
- A printed, completely filled-out copy of the AA4/8 form (the exact same one we linked in Path 1)
- _Extra:_ your post office receipt proving you have already applied for your Permesso di Soggiorno (if you already did it)

**3. Get your Italian Codice Fiscale**  
Once they process your paperwork, the turnaround is usually quite fast. In many cases, the clerk will print out your official paper _codice fiscale_ certificate right there on the spot. However, some Italian offices can be unpredictable. If their systems are down or they are incredibly backed up, they might tell you to come back the next day to pick it up. Stay flexible, but rest assured, the hard part is done\!

</GuideSteps>

## **Path 3: The Questura Route**

What happens if you arrive in Italy without a Codice Fiscale, and you genuinely do not need one right away? Maybe you are moving into a fully serviced corporate apartment with all utilities included, or you are moving in with a partner who already has an Italian bank account and handles all the household bills.

If you can afford to wait a month or two without needing to sign any official contracts, you can simply sit back and let the Italian bureaucracy do the work for you.

### **The Action Plan**

This is the most passive, automatic path available. You do not need to book any extra appointments at the tax office or fill out any additional tax forms.

<GuideSteps title="Path 3 action plan">

**1. File for your Permesso di Soggiorno**  
As a Digital Nomad or Remote Worker, you are legally required to go to the _Poste Italiane_ (the post office) and file your _Permesso di Soggiorno_ (residence permit) application kit within your first 8 days of arriving in Italy.

**2. Let the system run**  
When you submit that application kit, your data is sent to the _Questura_ (police headquarters). During their background checks and processing, the immigration system communicates directly with the _Agenzia delle Entrate_ behind the scenes to automatically generate a _Codice Fiscale_ for you.

**3. Receive your code with your permit**  
You do not have to do anything extra. When your _Permesso di Soggiorno_ is finally approved, and you are called back to the _Questura_ to pick up your physical residence permit card, your shiny new _Codice Fiscale_ will be printed right there on the plastic card.

</GuideSteps>

<GuideMistakeBox title="The downside of waiting">

While this path requires zero extra effort, you must remember that the Permesso di Soggiorno process is notoriously slow. It can easily take months between your initial Post Office visit and the day you actually get the card. If you rely on this automatic path, you will be living in Italy for months without a _Codice Fiscale_.

We generally suggest getting your code earlier so you aren't stuck if an emergency comes up, but if you have a solid support system in place, this "wait and see" route is completely valid.

</GuideMistakeBox>

# **Chapter 6 Where Will I Keep My Codice Fiscale?**

<GuideSpacer size="sm" />

Will you get a fancy plastic card right away? A digital barcode? Just a piece of paper?

Depending on which path you took above, your initial _Codice Fiscale_ might literally just be a printed PDF certificate from the Consulate or the _Agenzia delle Entrate_.

But as you settle into your Italian life, your 16-character code will eventually be officially printed on three major pieces of Italian plastic.

## **1. Your _Permesso di Soggiorno_ (Residence Permit)**

<GuideSoftTeal>

Once your residence permit is fully processed and approved, the _Questura_ (police headquarters) will hand you a physical, electronic card.  
**Your official Codice Fiscale is permanently printed right on the front of this card**.

Because you are legally required to carry your _Permesso di Soggiorno_ with you at all times as a foreigner in Italy, you will always have your Codice Fiscale in your pocket.

</GuideSoftTeal>

## **2. Your _Carta d'Identità Elettronica_ (CIE)**

<GuideSoftPurple title="Your digital-key card">

The _CIE (Carta d’Identità Elettronica)_ is the most important Italian document you need to get after the _Permesso di Soggiorno._  
The CIE acts as a digital key, letting you log into almost every single government and public service website in Italy. It is even mandatory to open a _Partita IVA_ _(Digital Nomads & freelancers, I’m talking to you)._

_We walk you through exactly how to get this important document in our Residence Registration add-on._

**Your _codice fiscale_ is also printed on that plastic card.**

</GuideSoftPurple>

## **3. The _Tessera Sanitaria_ (Health Card)**

If you speak to Italian locals about the Codice Fiscale, they will almost always assume you are talking about the _Tessera Sanitaria_, a blue plastic card issued by the public healthcare system.

For Italians, these two things are practically synonymous because the health card acts as the official, physical carrier of the CF. However, for internationals, it can be a bit different.

<GuideDecisionBox title="Important distinction for Digital Nomads and Remote Workers">

As a Digital Nomad or Remote Worker, you are required to have private health insurance for your visa, which means you are not automatically enrolled in the public Italian National Health Service (_Sistema Sanitario Nazionale_).

This is an incredibly important distinction: _you can absolutely live legally in Italy and have a valid Codice Fiscale without ever having a Tessera Sanitaria._

</GuideDecisionBox>

If you choose to voluntarily pay the annual fee to join the public healthcare system, you will receive the blue _Tessera Sanitaria_ in the mail. If you stick to your private insurance, you will not get one, and that is completely fine.

**Your _Permesso di Soggiorno_ and your _CIE_ are more than enough to prove your Codice Fiscale to anyone who asks.**

<GuideBigSis title="Big-sis tip">

Until you get your hard plastic Italian ID cards, keep a PDF copy of your original Agenzia delle Entrate/Consulate certificate on your phone, and download an app (like "Codice Fiscale" on iOS or Android) that generates a scannable barcode of your code.

</GuideBigSis>
