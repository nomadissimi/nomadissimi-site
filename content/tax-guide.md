## **Regime Ordinario: The One to Avoid If You Can (Digital Nomads)**

### **What Is It?**

If you move to Italy and do not specifically set up a special tax regime, the Regime Ordinario is what you will get stuck with by default. It is the standard, progressive tax system that applies to the vast majority of Italian taxpayers.

For freelancers (Digital Nomads), this means your _commercialista_ (accountant) opens a standard Partita IVA for you. For employees (Remote Workers), this is basically the standard tax bracket system that your company applies to your monthly paycheck. It is called "ordinary" because it is the ordinary way Italy taxes people. **But for internationals moving to Italy for a better life, it is definitely not the optimal choice.**

Under this regime, you are dealing with three main layers of taxation:

<GuideQuickFacts title="The 3 tax layers inside Regime Ordinario">

- **1. INPS (Social Security)**  
  **For Freelancers:** You are responsible for paying your own pension contributions to INPS, which sits at roughly 26.07% of your taxable income.  
  _We know that sounds like a big hit already (before even getting to the actual “tax”), but don't panic yet, we have dedicated a whole section to explaining how freelancer INPS actually works later in Chapter 4._

  **For Employees:** Your company automatically deducts your portion of social security directly from your gross salary before it ever hits your bank account.

- **2. IRPEF (National Income Tax) & Surcharges**  
  Whether you are a freelancer or an employee, your income is taxed progressively based on the following 2026 brackets:
  - Up to **€28,000** → **23%**
  - **€28,001 – €50,000** → **33%**
  - Above **€50,000** → **43%**

  _Plus a regional surtax of 1–3% and a municipal surtax up to 0.8%, varying by where you choose to live._

- **3. The IVA (Value Added Tax) — Freelancers Only**  
  If you are a freelancer operating under the Regime Ordinario, you cannot hide from the IVA (Italy's version of VAT). You are required to charge the standard 22% IVA on top of your invoices.

</GuideQuickFacts>

<GuideBigSis title="Important IVA reminder">

_IVA rules depend heavily on where your client is located, not where you are. We will cover exactly when you must charge IVA and when you are exempt later in this guide._

</GuideBigSis>

There is a silver lining to this entire regime. If you are in the Regime Ordinario, you are allowed to **deduct legitimate business or personal expenses from your taxable income** to lower the final amount you are taxed on (we will cover this in Chapter 5).

### **Why Should You Avoid It?**

<GuideMistakeBox title="Why this regime is painful">

Regime Ordinario adds up to be a heavy tax burden. When you combine the IRPEF brackets with INPS social security and regional surcharges, you are looking at losing roughly 40% to 50% of your gross income.

_Is there a real advantage?_ If you are a freelancer with incredibly high business expenses that offset your income significantly, the Regime Ordinario might occasionally compete with the other options.  
But for most Digital Nomads and Remote Workers, this is simply not the case.

_The golden rule:_ If you qualify for either the Regime Forfettario (freelancers) or the Regime Impatriati (freelancers and employees), the Regime Ordinario should be your absolute last choice.  
_Our goal is to keep you out of this bracket so you can keep your money for the dolce vita._

</GuideMistakeBox>

### **When You Might Actually Be Forced Into Regime Ordinario**

Sometimes, despite our best efforts, the Regime Ordinario is simply the only legal path available to you. You will find yourself in this tax bracket if:

<GuideChecklist title="When Ordinario becomes your only route">

- You hit the wealth ceiling: Your annual revenue is incredible and exceeds the €600,000 maximum limit for the Regime Impatriati _(although if this is your problem, congratulations lol)._

- _You are not technically a "new" resident:_ You have already been an Italian tax resident at some point during the past 3 years, which completely disqualifies you from claiming the Regime Impatriati incentive.

- You make a strategic choice _(Freelancers only)_: You decide not to use the Regime Forfettario because your actual business expenses (like heavy equipment, software, or travel) are so massive that deducting them under the Regime Ordinario actually saves you more money than the flat-tax rate.

</GuideChecklist>

### **The Magic of the 50% Exemption**

_How does taxing only 50% of your income affect your total taxation?_ Because Italy’s tax brackets are progressive, cutting your taxable income in half usually drops you into a much lower tax bracket.  
**This means you actually save more than just 50% on your tax bill.**

Here is a rough idea of how the _Regime Impatriati_ crushes the _Regime Ordinario_ at different income levels:

<GuideChecklist title="Orientative comparisons">

- **€40,000 annual gross income**
  - Regime Ordinario total tax burden: ~45%
  - Regime Impatriati total tax burden: ~22%

- **€80,000 annual gross income**
  - Regime Ordinario total tax burden: ~50%
  - Regime Impatriati total tax burden: ~22%

- **€150,000 annual gross income**
  - Regime Ordinario total tax burden: ~54%
  - Regime Impatriati total tax burden: ~24%

- **€200,000 annual gross income**
  - Regime Ordinario total tax burden: ~55%
  - Regime Impatriati total tax burden: ~26%

Now you can see the real power of the Regime Impatriati: even for very high incomes, the total taxation remains steadily and beautifully low\!

### **2026 Eligibility Requirements**

Italy doesn't hand out this incredible tax break to just anyone.  
To access the Regime Impatriati in 2026, you must meet ALL of these specific conditions:

<GuideChecklist title="Eligibility requirements">

- **Non-residence history:** You must NOT have been an Italian tax resident for at least the 3 consecutive tax years before moving to Italy.

- **New Italian tax resident:** You must officially become an Italian tax resident by moving your life here and registering your residency at the local Comune (_Anagrafe_).  
  _We guide you through your residency registration and all the other mandatory steps to settle in Italy inside our Residence Registration add-on._

- **Commitment to stay:** You must commit to remaining an Italian tax resident for at least 4 consecutive tax years.  
  If you leave before this period ends, you will lose the benefit and have to pay back the saved taxes with interest.

- **High qualification:** You must be highly qualified. Thankfully, if you hold the Digital Nomad or Remote Worker visa, you have already proven this to the consulate via your university degree or professional experience\!

- **Work performed in Italy:** You must spend the majority of the year (at least 183 days per year) actually living and performing your work activity in Italy.

- **Income cap:** The 50% exemption applies to a maximum annual income of €600,000.  
  If you make more than that, the excess is taxed under the standard regime.

- **You are a Freelancer who is exempt from IVA anyway:** The _Regime Impatriati_ usually requires you to manage IVA (Value Added Tax). However, depending on where your clients are located _(like B2B clients outside of Italy)_, you MIGHT be legally exempt from charging IVA anyway. If that is your case, the _Impatriati_ becomes a no-brainer.  
  _Don't worry, we will explain exactly how IVA works later in this guide\!_

</GuideChecklist>

## **Regime Forfettario: The Simplified Champion for Small Businesses _(Digital Nomads)_**

### **What Is It?**

If you are a Digital Nomad (freelancer) and you want **the easiest, most stress-free relationship with the Italian tax authorities**, the _Regime Forfettario_ is exactly what you are looking for.  
This is Italy’s flat-rate tax scheme designed specifically to protect small businesses from the crushing weight of standard bureaucracy. It delivers incredibly low taxes, drastically simplified accounting, and minimal headaches.

### **The Key Superpowers of the Forfettario**

<GuideChecklist title="Why people love it">

- **The 5% Flat Tax:** If you are starting a brand new business in Italy, your income tax rate is just 5% for the first 5 years. After that, it only goes up to 15%.  
  _Note: This is just your income tax; you still pay INPS social security on top, which we will cover shortly._

- **The €85,000 Shield:** You can stay in this low-tax bubble as long as your annual gross revenue stays under the €85,000 ceiling.

- **No IVA (VAT) Nightmares:** You are legally exempt from charging IVA on your invoices. This makes your services 22% cheaper for your private clients, and saves you from doing complicated VAT returns every quarter.

- **Minimal Bureaucracy:** Your bookkeeping is essentially just tracking the invoices you send out. You don't need to save every single coffee receipt to prove your expenses.

</GuideChecklist>

### **Can I Deduct Business Expenses?**

No, **you cannot deduct individual business expenses under this regime** (like your laptop, software, or work flights).

_But don’t panic:_ The government actually handles your expenses for you, automatically.

Instead of forcing you to prove every expense, **the government assumes a fixed "profit margin" for your business** based on your specific profession. This is called the **Profitability Coefficient**, and it is tied to your _ATECO Code_ (the official code that categorizes what you do for a living).

<GuideBigSis title="Big-sis translation">

Your ATECO code defines your job, and your Profitability Coefficient is the percentage of your revenue that the government considers "taxable profit." They assume the rest went toward your business expenses, so they don't tax it\!

</GuideBigSis>

### **Common ATECO Codes & Profitability Coefficients**

<GuideChecklist title="Common examples">

- **62.01.00** — Software development, programming → **67%**
- **74.10.10** — Graphic design → **78%**
- **62.20.10** — IT consulting, advisory on software/systems → **67%**
- **47.91.10** — Online retail, e-commerce → **40%**
- **73.11.00** — Advertising agencies → **78%**
- **72.11.00** — Software development → **78%**

</GuideChecklist>

### **How It Works: The Math**

Here is where the _Regime Forfettario_ shines. **Your taxable income is simply your Gross Revenue multiplied by your Profitability Coefficient.**

<GuideTaxSnapshot
title="Simple example"
subtitle="This is where the Forfettario starts to feel very attractive."

>

Let’s say you are an IT consultant (ATECO code 62.20.10, coefficient 67%) and you earn exactly €80,000 this year.

**Taxable Income = €80,000 × 67% = €53,600**

You only pay your 5% income tax and your INPS contributions on that €53,600. The government considers the other €26,400 to be your untaxed "business expenses," even if you only actually spent €200 on a web domain this year\!

</GuideTaxSnapshot>

<GuideMistakeBox title="Important warning">

Choosing the right ATECO code is a very delicate operation. A good commercialista will help you select the one that legally fits your job but offers the best coefficient.

</GuideMistakeBox>

### **2026 Eligibility: How to Get In (and Stay In)**

To qualify for the _Regime Forfettario_, you must meet these non-negotiable rules:

<GuideChecklist title="Forfettario rules">

- **The Revenue Ceiling:** Your annual revenue must not exceed €85,000.

- **The 5% Startup Rule:** To claim the magical 5% rate for your first 5 years, your new Italian business cannot just be a continuation of the exact same business activity you were doing in the previous 3 years.

- **The Mid-Year Trap:** If your revenue hits €100,000 during the year, you are kicked out of the Forfettario and forced into the heavy Regime Ordinario immediately.  
  However, you earn between €85,001 and €99,999, you get to finish the current year in the _Forfettario_, but you will be pushed into the _Ordinario_ the following year.

</GuideChecklist>

### **When Regime Forfettario Makes the Most Sense**

While the Regime Impatriati is incredibly powerful, the Regime Forfettario is often the undisputed winner for Digital Nomads if:

<GuideQuickFacts title="When Forfettario usually wins">

- **You are not eligible for the Regime Impatriati** (for example, if you already lived in Italy recently as a tax resident recently).

- **Your annual income is comfortably below the €85,000 threshold** and will likely stay there.

- **You have very few actual business expenses,** meaning the automatic coefficient gives you a bigger deduction than your real receipts would.

- You want the **simplest**, most **minimalist tax management** possible so you can spend your time enjoying Italy, not doing math.

- **You want to avoid charging IVA to your clients:** Depending on who your clients are and where they are located, the _Regime Impatriati_ might force you to add a 22% IVA (VAT) on top of your invoices. The _Forfettario_ legally exempts you from charging IVA entirely.

</GuideQuickFacts>

## **Wrapping It Up: The Big Three**

Alright, you have survived the heaviest part of Italian tax law. To quickly recap your options:

<GuideChecklist title="The big three">

- **Regime Ordinario**: The heavy, progressive default system. Avoid it if you legally can.

- **Regime Impatriati**: The powerhouse 50-60% tax exemption for newcomers. This is the ultimate path for Remote Workers (employees) and high-earning Digital Nomads.

- **Regime Forfettario**: The incredibly simple 5% flat tax with no IVA requirements, perfect for Digital Nomads earning under €85,000 who want a minimalist financial life.

</GuideChecklist>

## **INPS: How Freelancer Social Security Actually Works**

Let’s talk about the elephant in the room: _INPS: Istituto Nazionale della Previdenza Sociale_ (Italian social security institute).

When you start researching Italian taxes, _INPS_ is usually the acronym that causes the most panic. You see numbers like "26%" being thrown around and immediately assume the government is taking a chunk of your money just for fun.

<GuideSoftPurple title="What INPS actually is">

Let's reframe this. INPS is not a "tax" in the traditional sense; it is Italy’s mandatory public social security and pension system. The money you pay into INPS is essentially building your future retirement fund, while also providing you with public safety nets like maternity/paternity leave and sick pay.

</GuideSoftPurple>

### **The Gestione Separata (The Freelancer Fund)**

As a Digital Nomad, you don't fit into the traditional boxes of "lawyer" or "doctor," which have their own private pension funds. Instead, you will be enrolled in the INPS fund designed specifically for modern freelancers, consultants, and independent contractors: the _Gestione Separata_ (Separate Management Fund).

<GuideQuickFacts title="Why this fund is less scary than it sounds">

Here is the best part about the _Gestione Separata_: **it is 100% proportional to your income.** There are no scary fixed minimums. If you take a month off and earn zero euros, you pay zero INPS.

</GuideQuickFacts>

### **The Math: How Much Do You Pay?**

For 2026, the INPS rate for the Gestione Separata is officially set at **26.07%**.

But wait\! You do not pay 26.07% on every single euro you earn. **You only pay it on your taxable income.**

<GuideChecklist title="How the base changes depending on your regime">

- **If you are in the _Regime Forfettario_:**  
  You pay 26.07% only on the profit determined by your ATECO profitability coefficient.  
  For example, if your coefficient is 67%, you only pay INPS on 67% of your revenue.

- **If you are in the _Regime Impatriati_ / _Ordinario_:**  
  You pay 26.07% on your profit after deducting your real business expenses.

</GuideChecklist>

### **The Good Thing: INPS is Tax-Deductible**

<GuideTaxSnapshot
title="Why INPS is less painful than it first appears"
subtitle="This is the part most people miss."

>

Here is the thing that makes INPS much less painful: **the INPS contributions you pay this year are fully tax-deductible next year.**

Yes, even if you are in the strict _Regime Forfettario_ (which normally doesn't allow deductions), the Italian government makes a special exception for INPS. Before they calculate your income tax for the year, they subtract the exact amount of INPS you paid the previous year. This significantly lowers your taxable income, meaning paying your social security actually saves you money on your income tax down the line.

</GuideTaxSnapshot>

## **The IVA: When To Include It In Your Invoice And When Not**

If there is one topic that keeps Digital Nomads up at night, it is the _IVA_ (the Italian version of VAT).

The confusion is completely understandable. Sometimes you have to charge it, sometimes you don’t, and if you get it wrong, you end up owing the government money you never collected from your client.

But take a breath. The rules of IVA are actually based on one simple, logical concept: **The Principle of Territoriality** (_Principio di Territorialità_).

<GuideBlueNote title="The simple idea behind IVA">

The Italian government only wants to apply IVA to services that are actually "consumed" inside Italy.  
To figure out where a service is consumed, they look at two things: **who your client is, and where your client is located.**

</GuideBlueNote>

Let's look at the two main scenarios you will face as a Digital Nomad providing services (like consulting, design, or coding).

### **1. Selling to Businesses (The B2B Scenario)**

**B2B** stands for "**Business to Business**." This means your client is another registered business or freelancer, not a private individual.

When you (an Italian freelancer) provide a service to another business (B2B), the golden rule of Art. 7-ter of the Italian Tax Code kicks in: **the service is taxed in the country where the client is located.**

<GuideChecklist title="B2B rules">

- **If your B2B client is in Italy:**  
  You must **add the 22% Italian IVA** to your invoice _(unless you are in the Regime Forfettario, which makes you completely exempt from charging IVA)._

- **If your B2B client is outside Italy** (for example in the USA, UK, Chile, or Australia):  
  You **DO NOT charge Italian IVA.**  
  _Why?_ Because the client is not in Italy, so the service is not "consumed" in Italy.
  - **If they are in the EU:** They handle their own local VAT through a mechanism called Reverse Charge (_Inversione Contabile_). You just write "Reverse Charge" on your invoice.
  - **If they are outside the EU (e.g. USA):** It is completely outside the scope of European VAT. You just write _"Operazione non soggetta"_ (Operation not subject to VAT) on your invoice.

</GuideChecklist>

<GuideBigSis title="Why this matters so much">

This is a massive advantage\! If you are a freelancer with clients primarily in the US or UK, **you can use the Regime Impatriati to get the 50% income tax cut, AND you still won't have to charge them 22% IVA because they are foreign businesses.**

</GuideBigSis>

### **2. Selling to Private Individuals (The B2C Scenario)**

**B2C** stands for "**Business to Consumer**." This means your client is a normal, private person buying your service for their own personal use.

When you provide a service to a private individual, the territoriality rule flips: **the service is taxed in the country where the freelancer is located.**

It does not matter where the client lives: even if your private client lives in New York, the Italian government says, "You are in Italy, and the client isn't a business, so this service is taxed in Italy."

<GuideWarning title="The result">

**You MUST add 22% Italian IVA to your invoice**, unless you are protected by the _Regime Forfettario_.

</GuideWarning>

<GuideMistakeBox title="One exception to flag with your accountant">

There is a specific exception for automated digital services, like downloadable courses, e-books, or software subscriptions, sold to private clients in other EU countries. If your cross-border European sales exceed €10,000, the tax rules shift slightly.

If this sounds like your business model, make sure to flag it so your commercialista can track it for you. It’s always important to research if there are exceptions that might apply to your particular situation.

</GuideMistakeBox>

### **3. The E-Commerce Exception (Selling Physical Goods)**

_But wait, what if you aren't selling your knowledge or digital skills? What if you run an e-commerce store selling actual, physical products, like a line of artisan shoes?_

The rules we just talked about change entirely. Services are invisible, but physical goods have to actually cross international borders. Because of this, the **Italian tax agency cares less about who the client is (B2B vs. B2C), and much more about the map.**

For physical goods, the golden rule is based on logistics: **Where is the package starting from, and where is it landing?**

<GuideChecklist title="Physical goods rules">

- **If the goods stay in Italy:**  
  You charge the standard **22% Italian IVA**.

- **If the goods are shipped outside the EU** (like to a customer in Canada):  
  This is considered an official "export." You **DO NOT charge Italian IVA**, regardless of whether the buyer is a business or a private individual. The package will simply go through customs, and the buyer's country will handle any local taxes.

- **If the goods are shipped within the EU** (like to Portugal or Germany):  
  This is where e-commerce gets a bit spicy. The EU has a special system for this (called the OSS, or _One-Stop Shop_).  
  Generally, if you only sell a small amount across Europe (under €10,000 total), you just charge Italian IVA. But if your European sales blow up and cross that threshold, you will actually have to start charging the local VAT rate of the country where your customer lives.

</GuideChecklist>

<GuideBigSis title="E-commerce warning">

E-commerce IVA is its own entirely separate system heavily tied to shipping logistics, warehousing, and customs. If you are selling physical goods, you do not just need a standard accountant. Make sure you hire a _commercialista_ who specifically specializes in e-commerce and knows that world well.

</GuideBigSis>

### **Why You Need to Know This (And Why You Still Need a Commercialista)**

You might be thinking, _"If I'm going to hire an accountant, why do I need to memorize all this B2B and B2C stuff?"_

Because knowing this is exactly how you distinguish between a brilliant _commercialista_ and a mediocre one. Just because someone is an accountant in Italy doesn’t mean they’ll be the best fit for your particular situation. We learned that the hard way.

The rules we just covered are the general framework. However, the Italian tax code is full of golden loopholes and ultra-specific exceptions (known in tax-speak as the _Articolo 7_ exceptions) that depend entirely on _what_ exactly you do.

Here are a few classic examples that catch Digital Nomads off guard:

<GuideQuickFacts title="Examples of exceptions">

- **The "Nomad Services" Loophole:**  
  The government created a specific list of intellectual services that are completely exempt from Italian IVA when sold to a private individual (B2C) living outside the EU.  
  If you do technical consulting, marketing/advertising, data processing, or license your copyrights/IP, the rules suddenly flip. You get to charge 0% IVA to your private clients outside the EU.

- **The "Real Estate" Rule:**  
  If your remote work is tied to physical property (like remote architectural design, CAD engineering, or real estate consulting), the service is always taxed wherever the building is located, regardless of where your client lives.

- **The "In-Person Event" Rule:**  
  If you decide to host a real-life mastermind, retreat, or workshop in Italy, the tickets are taxed in Italy, even if every single attendee flew in from abroad.

</GuideQuickFacts>

Furthermore, to genuinely qualify for the standard tax-free B2B rules, the service you provide must be directly related to your client's actual business activity. (You cannot just bill a CEO's company for designing their personal wedding invitations\!)

<GuideMistakeBox title="What a mediocre accountant does vs a great one">

A mediocre _commercialista_ will just tell you to slap 22% IVA on everything to "be safe," making you unnecessarily expensive and uncompetitive to your foreign clients (and making you pay more in taxes unnecessarily). Instead, a great Italian accountant will dig into your specific business model, identify every legal exception your niche qualifies for, and protect you from charging IVA whenever legally possible.

</GuideMistakeBox>

<GuideBigSis title="What you should expect from your accountant">

Your _commercialista_ should be your strategic financial bodyguard, not just a tax collector. They should be updated with regimes, exceptions, and deadlines. If they don't ask you detailed questions about exactly what you sell and where your clients live during your very first meeting, it is time to find a new one.

</GuideBigSis>

## **What Does It Mean to Deduct Business Expenses? _(For Digital Nomads)_**

**A tax deduction simply means reducing the amount of money the government is allowed to tax by subtracting your legitimate business expenses from your total income.**

<GuideTaxSnapshot
title="The concept in action"
subtitle="This is the basic logic behind deductions."

>

- **Gross Income:** You earned €100,000 this year.
- **Deductible Business Expenses:** You spent €30,000 running your freelance business.
- **Taxable Income:** €100,000 − €30,000 = €70,000.
- **The Result:** You only pay taxes on €70,000, not €100,000.

That gap is where you win\!

</GuideTaxSnapshot>

Every legitimate business expense you track and deduct means fewer euros get taxed. Over a year, keeping track of your receipts can easily mean thousands of euros saved, money that stays in your pocket to fund your next flight, rather than going into the tax bill.

### **Who is Allowed to Deduct Expenses?**

Your ability to deduct expenses depends entirely on your tax regime:

<GuideChecklist title="Who can deduct what?">

- **Regime Ordinario:** Yes\! You can deduct your legitimate business expenses.

- **Regime Impatriati:** Yes\! You deduct your business expenses _first_, and then the famous 50% tax exemption is applied to whatever is left.  
  _(This is the ultimate combo\!)_

- **Regime Forfettario:** NO. In this flat-tax regime, the government gives you a fixed, automatic "flat rate" deduction based on your profession. You cannot deduct your actual, real-life expenses.

</GuideChecklist>

### **The Golden Rule: What Can a Digital Nomad Actually Deduct?**

If you are in the _Regime Ordinario_ or _Regime Impatriati,_ you can deduct expenses that follow the Italian **principle of _inerenza_**. This simply means the expense must be directly related to your business activity.

<GuideSoftPurple title="The mindset shift">

The beauty of deductions is that they aren’t a rigid, boring menu. With the right mindset _(and a brilliant commercialista)_, you can turn many of your daily nomad costs into legal tax savings.

</GuideSoftPurple>

Here are the most common deductions for Digital Nomads:

<GuideQuickFacts title="Common deductible categories">

- **Tech & Equipment:**  
  Laptops, extra monitors, keyboards, microphones, and camera gear used for your business.

- **Software & Subscriptions:**  
  Adobe Creative Cloud, Zoom, Slack, website hosting, domain names, and cloud storage.

- **The "Home Office" Setup:**  
  If you work from your Italian apartment, you can often deduct up to 50% of your rent, electricity, and internet bills, because it doubles as your "office" \!

- **Professional Services:**  
  Yes, the fees you pay to your commercialista are deductible.

- **Marketing & Growth:**  
  Paid ads, courses, professional certifications, and industry books.

- **Nomad Travel & Transport:**  
  This is the tricky one. Flights, hotels, and restaurant meals while traveling for business are deductible, but usually only up to 75% of the cost, and with strict annual limits. However, if you travel for a specific client and bill those exact travel costs directly to them on your invoice, they don't count toward your taxable income at all.

</GuideQuickFacts>

<GuideMistakeBox title="Do not guess deduction percentages">

_Big-sis tip:_ Not every expense is deductible at 100%. The Italian tax code assigns different deductible percentages depending on the type of cost (for example, a business dinner is treated differently than buying a new laptop).

**Never guess.** Keep every single invoice, send them all to your commercialista, and let them figure out exactly how to maximize your savings under the current tax law.

</GuideMistakeBox>
